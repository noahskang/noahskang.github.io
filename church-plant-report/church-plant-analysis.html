§ I. The Question

What's the shape of the call?

A bivocational team of ten, gathered from across the country. An Asian-American leadership shape. The pull of a major university campus and a vision for a midweek young-adult gathering at scale. These are the gifts the Lord has placed in our hands.

The question is not where will this team succeed, but where is the Lord sending us — which city's spiritual need most fits the shape of this calling, and where can we give our lives so that disciples are made, formed, and sent for the long haul.

This document began as a working comparison of two strong candidates — Atlanta and Phoenix — with a proposal for a Midtown-anchored start that took seriously what God was already doing in the metro, including a sister AA-led plant already on the field. It has since expanded to include Charlotte (a Carolina-pipeline-live banking city with a particular openness to the team's posture) and New York / Downtown Brooklyn (a multi-campus, post-Christian field where the gospel is faintly heard among young adults despite the city's religious surface). Several additional candidates were prayed over and set aside; those are surfaced in the Alternatives tab with the structural reason each was set aside.

§ III. The Sending Question

Do they stay?

Three sending paths come out of every graduating class — local bivocational, plant elsewhere, missions. The first path requires that graduates stay in the metro; the other two do not. So whether students stay does not decide whether the discipleship arc bears fruit; it decides what share of that fruit roots locally and becomes the church's own sending base, and what share is sent further out.

Atlanta — retention is structural.

The Tech "Yellow Jacket Mafia" is not a meme. Tech alone supports an estimated 33,000 Georgia jobs and contributes ~$5.3B to the state's economy annually. Atlanta hosts 17 Fortune 500 headquarters — Coca-Cola, Delta, Home Depot, UPS, Cox, Inspire Brands, Genuine Parts, NCR, Equifax, Aflac, Truist, Southern Co. — alongside major Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, and Mailchimp/Intuit footprints. Emory anchors a healthcare cluster with the CDC across the street.

  • Tech + Emory grads recruited heavily by local F500 / Big Tech ATL offices
  • Cost still permits new grads to live in Midtown / O4W / West Midtown
  • Deep young-professional culture: people stay 3 – 7 years post-grad
  • What this means for the church: students discipled at 19 can become bivocational co-laborers at 23 — in the same metro, gathered into the local sending base

Phoenix — retention is in transition.

Historically, ASU grads trended toward LA, Seattle, Austin, and the Bay Area for higher-paying tech work. That picture is changing rapidly: TSMC's Phoenix investment program (announced in stages from $40B in 2022 to roughly $165B by 2025), Intel's Ocotillo expansion, the broader East Valley semiconductor corridor, Amazon's massive operations footprint, and a swelling finance/insurance presence are creating reasons to stay that didn't exist five years ago.

  • AAPI tech grads increasingly held by Chandler corridor employers
  • Cheap rent makes staying viable for 22-year-olds with internships only
  • But: post-grad ecosystem still less mature than Atlanta or DFW
  • What this means for the church: the local-bivocational path is thinner here — the plant-elsewhere and missions paths still run normally, and Phoenix may be a sending city more than a settling one

Charlotte — students stay, and more come.

Charlotte may be where the most graduates stay of any city in this set. UNCC alumni cluster heavily inside the metro by default — UNCC is overwhelmingly an in-state, in-region school — and the banking sector (BoA, Truist, Wells Fargo, JPMorgan Chase Charlotte) recruits both directly from UNCC and from UNC, Duke, and other regional schools, drawing more young adults into the metro than UNCC itself sends out. Post-pandemic NYC and Bay-Area finance migration brings another stream of young adults into the city.

  • UNCC's in-state student body stays in-region by default
  • Banking concentration (BoA HQ + Truist HQ) draws finance careers
  • The metro takes in more young pros than it sends out
  • What this means for the church: the local-bivocational path is the deepest of any candidate — Charlotte's discipleship arc can extend over both the students who stay and the young adults who arrive

New York — the three-year arc, and the rooted few.

Brooklyn's young-adult retention is the most complicated of the four. A meaningful share of the under-30 imports — finance analysts, design first-jobs, tech transplants — give the city two or three good years and then leave for Austin, Charlotte, or the suburbs. That is the city's gravity, not the church's failure. Countervailing the transience: LIU's commuter base is rooted, many AA young adults moving into Fort Greene and Bed-Stuy are coming home rather than leaving home, and Pratt graduates often stay in Brooklyn's creative ecosystem for a decade or more.

  • Three-year young-pro arc is real for the imported finance / tech population
  • LIU + Pratt + native AA community provide a meaningful rooted base
  • Sending paths (plant elsewhere, missions, family-formation in another metro) carry equal weight to local rootedness
  • What this means for the church: a body that stays for ten years is the structural counter to Brooklyn's churn — patient week-after-week presence is the only thing that builds something people might actually stay in

00The Field of Need

A Bible-Belt city quietly dechurching a generation.

Atlanta wears its religious identity on the surface — 65–70% of metro residents identify as Christian (Pew), churches sit on most major intersections in the city and suburbs, and "going to church" still carries default-cultural weight in a way it does not in Phoenix or New York. The honest read of the under-30 picture is harder. Among Atlanta young adults, particularly the Tech / Midtown / Old Fourth Ward / West Midtown population the plant would be sent to, the dominant story is quiet drift — Southern evangelical kids who left the suburban church of their parents and have not found one to replace it; AA young adults whose grandparents' Black-church memory does not translate into Sunday-morning belonging; international and AAPI students whose parents' faith was inherited rather than confessed. The vocabulary of the gospel is familiar here. It is precisely that familiarity that has dulled it.

The dominant spiritual register among Tech and Emory undergraduates is achievement-as-meaning — engineering culture, pre-med, the Big Tech / consulting / banking funnel. Young professionals settling in Midtown layer career striving with secular wellness, therapy language, and a low-grade suspicion of organized religion that often started in a youth-group disappointment they have not narrated. The Black-church tradition is still vibrant in pockets but has its own dechurching crisis among the under-30 cohort, especially as that generation moves into majority-multi-ethnic neighborhoods like O4W and West Midtown. Cultural Christianity remains the warm baseline; lived faith is thinner than the baseline suggests.

Faithful churches are at work — the verdict and Block 04 name them. What is missing is not a young-adult church but the particular shape this team is built to be: an AA-led, integrated college-and-young-pro body that takes the Tech-to-Midtown discipleship arc as one continuous formation rather than two demographics, and that holds Black-church cultural memory with multi-ethnic table-fellowship without making either a gate. That pairing is genuinely thin in Atlanta's existing ministry landscape.

Why this team, here. The Bible-Belt context means the gospel will be heard in familiar vocabulary, even where it must be reclaimed from cultural Christianity. The team's AA-led leadership shape and integrated college-to-young-pro architecture are not what existing Atlanta churches are doing; they are the obedience this field is asking for. Sister-A2N work in the metro is a feature — a brother work, not a competitor — and shapes how this plant should walk in.

01Campus Density

Tech is the anchor. The rest is upside.

Atlanta's multi-campus reach is real, but more limited than the headline number suggests. Georgia Tech is the obvious centerpiece — 47,000 students, walking distance to Midtown — and is the one campus the plant must plan around. Beyond Tech, the rest is grace, not premise.

SCAD Atlanta sits inside Midtown itself: a small but culturally adjacent student body the plant can love without extra logistics. Georgia State's downtown campus is one MARTA stop away, but its student profile skews older and commuter-heavy — a different demographic than an undergrad-formation work. Emory is a 15–25 minute drive east depending on traffic; reaching its roughly 7,000 undergraduates from a Midtown gathering is a stretch on most weeknights, and Emory already has well-developed religious-life ministries the plant should honor rather than duplicate. AUC is closer geographically but represents a distinct institutional and cultural world that an outside team should not assume it can serve.

The honest frame: plan for Tech-and-SCAD on a Tuesday night. GSU, Emory, and AUC become live questions only if the Lord opens doors and the team has the bandwidth to walk through them. Designing for the focused version is more honest than promising a five-campus reach the team cannot deliver.

03Asian American Reality

Mature, dense, and twenty miles away.

Atlanta has roughly twice the Asian American density of Phoenix metro and one of the fastest-growing AAPI communities in the South. Between 2013 and 2019 metro Atlanta added more than 75,000 AAPI residents — the 10th-largest absolute increase among US metros.

The catch: that density lives in Gwinnett (Duluth 22%, Suwanee 24%) and North Fulton (Johns Creek 29%). It is not in Midtown. The team will live, worship, and break bread in two different worlds — campus / Midtown by day, Buford Highway / Duluth on weekends. This is workable, and arguably a gift, but it has to be designed for.

06A sustainable rhythm

A liveable city for ten bivocational families — though not cheap.

Midtown 1BR rent runs $1,900 – $2,200; West Midtown and Old Fourth Ward shave 10–15% off. Home Park and points west get cheaper still. Bivocational work is genuinely abundant — Tech, Microsoft, Google ATL, Mailchimp/Intuit, Salesforce, Coca-Cola, Delta, Home Depot, NCR, Cox, Inspire Brands, plus the Emory / CDC / Emory Healthcare cluster on the east side. The team can find work that lets them give their lives here for the long haul.

07The Carolina pipeline

A provided network the team already has.

Several of the team did college ministry at UNC. That's not a footnote — it's a gift the Lord has already placed in this team's hands, one that travels well to Atlanta in a way it doesn't to Phoenix. Chapel Hill is a 6.5-hour drive or a 90-minute direct flight from Atlanta. There are daily nonstops. UNC has roughly 32K students, a deep evangelical campus-ministry tradition, and an alumni population in metro Atlanta that runs into the tens of thousands.

Three downstream implications:

i.
Co-laborers in years two through five.   UNC ministry alumni — the tier just behind the launch ten — are far more likely to consider moving to Atlanta than to Phoenix. The team that joins the work in years two through five has a real pool to be drawn from in prayer and friendship.
ii.
Summer pipeline back to Chapel Hill.   Easy summer trips for recruiting Carolina seniors who'd otherwise scatter. Easy joint events with whoever's still doing campus ministry there. Easy intern exchanges.
iii.
Future sister plants.   The same network that the Lord uses to feed the Atlanta plant could one day seed future Southeast plants — Charlotte, Nashville, Raleigh-Durham — without starting recruiting from nothing.

From Phoenix, this advantage essentially disappears. Chapel Hill to Phoenix is a five-hour flight with a layover; the casual drive-or-fly cadence that makes UNC a live partner from Atlanta becomes a planned, expensive logistics problem from the desert.

§ VI. The Midtown Strategy

Plant in Midtown.
Bridge campus and city.

A college-and-young-professional church anchored in Midtown Atlanta — built first around Georgia Tech, with SCAD Atlanta as natural adjacency, and embedded in the post-grad neighborhood where 22–30s already live, work, and look for community. GSU is reachable by MARTA and Emory by car, but those are secondary work, not the premise.

— Pillar 01 —

Sunday gathering.
Midweek front door.

Sunday morning is for the church family — undergrads, young pros, a few young families. Tuesday or Wednesday night is the public-facing gathering: large, polished, evangelistic, where students bring friends.

— Pillar 02 —

College ministry as a
core arm.

Not a feeder, not an afterthought. A dedicated college pastor, weekly college community, semester-paced rhythms, fall and spring retreats. Students should know this church is for them — not just a stepping-stone to the young-pro thing.

— Pillar 03 —

Young-pro ministry as the
parallel arm.

Vocational discipleship, neighborhood small groups in West Midtown / O4W / Buckhead, marriage and family on-ramps, third-place hospitality. The rhythm of life after 22 is structurally different from college; programming has to reflect that.

— Pillar 04 —

AA sensibility,
multi-ethnic table.

Cultural touchstones, food, hospitality, leadership shape — but not a membership requirement. Tech and Midtown are too multi-ethnic to do anything else; the AA-led identity is the flavor, not the gate.

The six-year discipleship arc.

The arc is faster than it sounds. A student arrives as a freshman and is welcomed in. By sophomore or junior year the Lord forms them into a leader. They graduate ready to serve. Within a year or two of graduation they are functioning as a kingdom worker — locally, in another US city, or overseas. Six years from welcome to sending.

Tactic 01 — Venue

Anchor near Tech Square.

Walking distance for Georgia Tech and SCAD Atlanta students; one MARTA stop from GSU's downtown campus. Emory sits at the edge of practical reach for a Midtown gathering — plan for it as occasional, not standing. Either rent a midweek venue (Tabernacle, Center Stage, Variety Playhouse, hotel ballrooms) or partner with an existing Midtown church for shared space.

Cost order — $4–8K / month rented · $0 partnership
Tactic 02 — Cadence

Tuesday is the front door.

Worship + teaching + invitation-friendly, designed so undergrads can bring friends. Sunday is a smaller, family-feel gathering for members and regulars. Midweek can be larger than Sunday by design.

Pattern — large gathering + church family
Tactic 03 — College Pastor

Hire a college pastor from day one.

Not a "young adult" pastor who treats college as a sub-population. A dedicated leader for the 18-22 ministry: weekly college community, an embedded presence at Georgia Tech first (with SCAD as natural adjacency), fall/spring retreats, mentoring pipelines into the church's young-pro arm. GSU and Emory presence becomes a year-two question, only if a willing student leader emerges.

Lead — within first 12 months

Team lives in Midtown / West Midtown / O4W.

Bivocational families need to be neighbors to the people they're reaching. Avoid the Duluth-commute trap that breaks most ATL young-adult ministries. Rent comes with the territory.

06The GCU Pipeline

A 25,000-student Christian campus down the road.

Twenty-five minutes from Tempe sits a structural asset most planters overlook: Grand Canyon University. 25,000 traditional residential students, the largest residential population of any private university in the country. Founded in 1949 as a Christian college, still explicitly Christian today, with a curriculum built around Christian-worldview formation and a campus culture that pushes against the broader cultural tide of college-age deconstruction.

The national pattern is the one to sit with: research from Barna, Lifeway, and Pew consistently shows that a majority of churched students — typically 60–70% by graduation — disengage from active Christian faith during their college years. GCU's internal surveys show the inverse: roughly 9 in 10 students report their faith is supported and developing through their time on campus. Those students are not the same evangelistic field as ASU. They are something else entirely.

i.
Mature leaders, not first-time seekers.   A church plant in Phoenix can welcome GCU students as student leaders — running small groups, leading worship, doing campus outreach at ASU — in their second or third year, with theology classes already done.
ii.
Co-laborers in years two through five.   GCU graduates considering ministry, missions, or the marketplace are already pointed toward kingdom work. They are exactly the brothers and sisters a bivocational team needs to be drawn into the work in years 2–5.
iii.
Sending base.   Many GCU students arrive intending to plant or go overseas. A church that can be their home for 4–6 years and then send them well is positioned to feed the second and third paths of the discipleship arc — plant elsewhere, missions — even where fewer graduates stay locally.

What's actually on the GCU campus.   The pipeline thesis lands harder once you see GCU's own ministry footprint up close. Three weekly chapel services average roughly 5,000 attendees combined. Life Groups — weekly student-led Bible studies — run in every residential living area on campus, staffed by five professional Spiritual Life staff and ~220 trained student leaders. The Center for Worship Arts feeds Canyon Worship and trains worship leaders by the cohort. A Christian-worldview core curriculum runs across all programs. External para-church organizations (Cru, RUF, InterVarsity) are functionally minimal on campus — the institution itself does that work.

The implication cuts both ways. Positively: GCU is genuinely producing trained student ministry leaders at scale, and a plant that lands a partnership with even a modest share of that flow has a real talent pipeline. Honestly, though, two frictions deserve to be named up front. Bandwidth — most upperclassmen who would be leadership candidates are already embedded in Life Group leadership, worship team, or outreach roles on their own campus, so off-campus recruitment is competing with existing investment, not filling empty time. Distance — 25 minutes to Tempe is fine for a Sunday or a major event, harder for casual mid-week presence. The pipeline is real; it is not friction-free, and the Block 08 strategy assumes a formal partnership channel rather than ad-hoc recruiting precisely because of these two.

The role-split is structurally clean: ASU is the mission field; GCU is the leadership pipeline. A weekly gathering near Tempe reaches the unchurched 80K; intentional partnership with GCU recruits and trains the leaders who reach them. This dual-anchor logic doesn't exist in Atlanta with anything like the same shape.

00The Field of Need

Among the least-reached young adults in North America.

Brooklyn is one of the most densely populated, most spiritually scattered concentrations of young adults on the continent. Evangelical density across the borough sits in the low single digits — most counts hover around 3 to 4 percent — while the population it would have to reach is the largest urban under-40 cohort in the country. The math is a missional one before it is a strategic one: the gospel is least heard in the rooms most full of people who need to hear it.

The cultural mood is not the simple secularism of a generation ago. New Yorkers are not unspiritual. Pew's own work has long shown the city scoring high on religious identity — Catholic heritage, Jewish heritage, immigrant Pentecostalism, Orthodoxy of several traditions — alongside one of the lowest weekly-practice rates of any major metro. The dominant under-30 register in Brooklyn is the SBNR pattern: spiritual but not religious, formed by therapy language, secular wellness, climate grief, identity politics, and a low-grade post-Christian disillusionment with whatever institutional faith their parents held. Lapsed Catholic. Lapsed Jewish. Lapsed evangelical refugee from the suburbs. Many were close enough to church once that "religion" carries a charge — which is its own missional reality.

What is missing in the current ministry coverage is not a lack of churches — there are good ones, named honestly below. What is thin is steady, integrated, AA-led discipleship that gathers the four Downtown-Brooklyn campuses into one body, holds them next to young professionals giving their lives in the same neighborhoods, and stays long enough for those rhythms to become formation. Most college work in the city is para-church and runs alongside churches rather than inside them. Most young-adult churches in Brooklyn are doing real work but were not planted with a college arm in their architecture from day one. The shape of the need is the shape of this team's calling.

This is a team of ten — bivocational, AA-led — built to give their lives in one place over a long arc. Brooklyn does not need another launch. It needs a body that stays. The work the team is built for — long-term rootedness, a plurality of elders, local teaching, college and young-pro held together — is the work this field has been quietly waiting on.

02Neighborhood Geography

One walking radius, five neighborhoods, every subway line.

The geography is the gift here. Downtown Brooklyn proper, Brooklyn Heights, DUMBO, Fort Greene, and Clinton Hill form a continuous pedestrian arc roughly two miles end to end. Within that arc sits one of the densest subway nodes in the city — the Jay St–MetroTech / Hoyt-Schermerhorn / Atlantic Ave–Barclays cluster aggregates the A, C, F, R, 2, 3, 4, 5, B, Q, D, N, and G — meaning a Sunday gathering anchored anywhere in the arc is one transfer or fewer from most of Brooklyn and most of lower Manhattan.

  • Downtown Brooklyn — Tandon (MetroTech), corporate offices, a working civic core; not where people linger, but where they arrive every weekday.
  • Brooklyn Heights — old brownstones, the promenade, families and long-time residents; quiet, mature, churched (relatively) compared to the rest of the arc.
  • DUMBO — tech offices, design studios, St. Francis's new home; cobblestones and a young-pro waterfront register, with an outsized concentration of media and creative workers.
  • Fort Greene — historically Black middle-class Brooklyn, BAM (Brooklyn Academy of Music), Fort Greene Park, brownstone density, deep cultural memory; a neighborhood whose history matters to an AA-led plant.
  • Clinton Hill — Pratt's neighborhood, brownstones interleaved with newer development, an arts-and-grad-student feel that bleeds into Bed-Stuy on its eastern edge.

Where young adults gather: Devoción and Café Grumpy in Downtown Brooklyn; Brooklyn Roasting Company in DUMBO; Greenlight Bookstore and Habana Outpost (when open) in Fort Greene; Sincerely, Tommy and the cluster around Pratt in Clinton Hill; the BRIC house and BAM campus for Friday-and-weekend rhythms; Fort Greene Park and Brooklyn Bridge Park for warm-weather third-place life. 04Rootedness & Sending

Brooklyn's young-adult transience is real. So is the call to stay.

Anyone who has worked in young-adult ministry in New York knows the ache of the three-year arc. A 24-year-old arrives for a finance analyst program or a design first-job; gives two or three good years to a community; gets a senior role in Austin or has a kid and moves to New Jersey or burns out on the city and leaves. The retention math is not the church's failure. It is the city's gravity.

The team's calling does not flinch from this. The discipleship arc the team is built for assumes some people will be sent and some people will stay, and that both are good. The fruit being prayed toward is not a count of attenders held in the room. It is people formed long enough to be sent well — into vocational rhythm where they are, into a plant elsewhere, into missions, into families and neighborhoods and workplaces in this city. A church that disciples someone for three Brooklyn years and sends them faithfully into a Phoenix marriage or a Charlotte plant is doing the work it was built to do.

And — countervailing the transience read — there is a meaningful population in Brooklyn that does stay. LIU's commuter base is rooted here. Many AA young adults moving into Fort Greene and Bed-Stuy are coming home, not leaving home. Pratt graduates often stay in the borough's creative ecosystem for a decade or more. The work of patient, named, week-after-week presence is not wasted on a transient field — it is the only thing that builds the kind of body people might actually stay in. A team that gives its life here for ten years, not three, is the structural counter to Brooklyn's churn.

05Bivocational Runway

The cost is severe. Naming it is the first faithful step.

There is no soft version of this section. New York is the most expensive bivocational field in the candidate set — meaningfully more expensive than Atlanta, Phoenix, or Charlotte. A 1BR within the Downtown Brooklyn arc runs $3,200–$4,200; pushing east into Bed-Stuy or south into Crown Heights brings that closer to $2,400–$3,000 for the same square footage. Two-incomes-and-roommates is the norm, not the exception, well into the team's late twenties. Childcare in Brooklyn ranges $24K–$36K per child per year. A bivocational team of ten committing to give their lives here long-term has to walk into this with eyes open and a specific runway, not vague optimism.

What sustainability actually looks like:

  • Tech / engineering — Brooklyn-based tech offices (Etsy in DUMBO, Squarespace, Vimeo); contracting and remote roles for Manhattan-headquartered firms; Tandon faculty / staff adjacencies.
  • Finance support roles — operations, compliance, technology-in-banking at the Manhattan F500 base; Goldman, JPMorgan, Citi, Morgan Stanley all hold meaningful Brooklyn-commutable workforces.
  • Education — NYC DOE (the largest school district in the country), independent schools (Saint Ann's, Packer, Brooklyn Friends, Berkeley Carroll), Tandon / LIU adjunct teaching.
  • Healthcare — NewYork-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist, NYU Langone, Maimonides; nursing roles especially deep through LIU's pipeline.
  • Creative freelance — design, media, editorial; real but cyclical, requires savings discipline to fit a discipleship rhythm.

A realistic plan pre-places jobs for all ten before launch and accepts that the household geometry of a Brooklyn bivocational team will look different from a Charlotte or Phoenix one — more roommates, more shared housing, more delayed family-formation milestones. The cost of admission is real. The team is being asked to give their lives here for the long haul, in a field that is among the least-reached in the country. The runway, planned with care, can carry that weight. Pretending the cost is lower than it is would be the failure.

06The Two-Wing Bridge

Undergrads mentoring high-schoolers; young pros anchoring the body. One church, two arms reinforcing each other.

The distinctive shape of this plant in Brooklyn is the way two natural ministries — a college arm across four campuses and a young-pro arm across Manhattan and Downtown Brooklyn employers — bridge into a third: youth outreach into Brooklyn high schools through undergraduate mentors. Each arm gives the others something they would not otherwise have.

The young-professional wing. Members in finance (Manhattan F500), tech (DUMBO + Manhattan), media (Brooklyn Navy Yard, DUMBO, Manhattan), design (Pratt-adjacent ecosystem, DUMBO), and healthcare (NewYork-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist, NYU Langone) form the adult center of the body. They host neighbors, share faith in their workplaces, walk through marriage and singleness and grief in front of college students who have never seen Christian adulthood lived in their city. This is the formation a college student cannot get from a college ministry alone — and is the structural distinctive carried over from the team's Distinctive I.

The college wing. A college pastor with explicit responsibility for all four campuses. A weekly college community on rotating campus locations or a single Downtown-Brooklyn meeting site within walking distance of three of the four. Embedded presence at Tandon (the largest), LIU (the most rooted), Pratt (the most culturally influential), with St. Francis as the natural Catholic-heritage adjacency. Retreats. Mentoring tracks. Real connection into the body's Sunday gathering, where college students see what comes next.

The youth-outreach bridge. Brooklyn high schools are dense, diverse, and underserved by relational mentorship — a missional reality across Bed-Stuy, Fort Greene, Crown Heights, and Brownsville. The team's undergrads — many themselves only a few years out of high school, formed in the church's college community — become the natural mentors for Brooklyn 14-to-18-year-olds, through one or two named partnerships (a single after-school program, a single church-school partnership) rather than a sprawl. The young-pro wing supports financially and with adult presence at events. The college wing supplies relational consistency. The youth-outreach work feeds future college students into the campus ministry. The discipleship arc, in this city, runs from 14 to 24 to 34, in one body, in one neighborhood, over a decade.

This shape is not a clever positioning. It is the shape of a church that takes the team's three distinctives seriously — whole-city not college-only, plural eldership, local rooted teaching — and lets them work themselves out in a Brooklyn-specific geography.

  • The four-campus shape spreads the college pastor thin in year one — Tandon and LIU primary, Pratt and St. Francis adjacencies, with deliberate sequencing required
  • Sunday-rhythm logistics in NYC are not what they are in Charlotte — venue cost, scheduling around city events, transit-rather-than-parking, all add operational friction
§ X. Alternatives Considered

Eleven cities not chosen — and why.

Eleven alternatives surfaced across the search. Each was set aside for a specific structural reason — Richmond is first because it was in the candidate set until just being moved here.

— 01 · Mid-Atlantic —

Richmond · VCU

Cleanest geometry of any candidate — undermined by ceiling and adjacency.
  • VCU R1, ~29K undergrad, in-city walkable from Monroe Park
  • Richmond metro 1.3M; eight F500 HQs (Altria, CarMax, Markel, Dominion, etc.) plus Capital One West Creek
  • Fewest existing young-adult ministries of any candidate considered — meaningful relational space for a new body in five years
  • Cheapest cost of living after Phoenix; usable Carolina pipeline (3.5h drive)
  • A2N-open in Richmond proper; UVA / Axiom 1h west
Why set aside Smallest absolute college-and-young-pro field of the candidates — even reaching the whole of young-adult Richmond touches fewer people than reaching one neighborhood of Atlanta. UVA / Axiom adjacency 1h west is the closest sister-plant geography of any candidate. The team closed the working set to Atlanta, Phoenix, Charlotte, and New York; Richmond is the most serious alternative if one of those four falls out.
— 02 · Bay Area —

Berkeley · UC Berkeley

The strongest demographic and academic match — the only candidate fully on-criterion.
  • Top-3 public university in the US, ~33K undergrad, ~38% AAPI
  • Bay Area metro 7.7M — densest US Asian-American metro
  • A2N already at Cal (Acts 2 Fellowship, formerly Klesis, rebranded 2023)
  • The team would be a re-plant entering an active public conversation about the existing AA-led incumbent
Why set aside Brand-distinction work with the existing operation is structural. Different decision shape than the four green-field candidates; the team chose to close the set without it. Worth revisiting if the brand-distinction work becomes part of the calling.
— 03 · Texas —

Dallas-Fort Worth · UT Dallas + SMU

Closest non-Berkeley match on AAPI density and metro size.
  • UT Dallas — R1 since 2014, AAU 2018, ~25K undergrad, ~30% Asian — closest A2N-open AAPI undergrad share to Cal's
  • SMU top-50 private (~7K), TCU, UT Arlington round out the multi-campus
  • DFW metro 8M (4th-largest US), Plano/Frisco 25-30% Asian
  • Deepest non-coastal bivocational job market in America (finance, telecom, defense, tech, consulting)
  • A2N-open
Why set aside Texas Bible Belt baseline is real; not "unreached" by Bay Area standards. UT Dallas is rising-mid-tier rather than flagship-elite. Carolina pipeline inert.
— 04 · Texas —

Houston · Rice + UH

Second-largest US metro the team could pick — AAPI-dense, A2N-open.
  • Rice top-15 private but tiny (~4K undergrad)
  • University of Houston R1 (~46K, ~30% Asian undergrad), top-100 public
  • Houston metro 7.1M (5th-largest US), Sugar Land 50%+ Asian, Bellaire and Pearland adjacent
  • Energy + medical center + aerospace corporate base
  • A2N-open
Why set aside Rice is elite-tier but too small to anchor; UH is mid-tier rather than flagship; many large churches are already at work in the city (Lakewood, Champion Forest, Houston's First); Bible Belt baseline strong.
— 05 · Florida —

Gainesville · University of Florida

Flagship academic anchor — undermined by metro retention.
  • UF top-15 public, R1, AAU member, ~35K undergrad — meets the academic-seriousness bar fully
  • A2N-open
  • Mid-Carolina pipeline geometry (~8h drive, 90-min flight to RDU)
  • Gainesville metro is small (~140K)
Why set aside The Phoenix question of how many graduates stay, in extreme form. UF graduates scatter to Tampa, Orlando, Jacksonville, Atlanta, and Miami within twelve months of graduation. Gainesville itself is too small to hold a young-pro post-grad population long enough for a discipleship arc to take root.

Blacksburg · Virginia Tech

Strongest engineering anchor in the Gainesville-shape category.
  • VT top-50 national, top-15 engineering, AAU member · ~30K undergrad
  • ~9–11% AAPI undergrad · A2N-open
  • Drivable Carolina pipeline (3.5h to Chapel Hill, 3h to Charlotte)
  • Blacksburg ~45K, MSA ~165K · Roanoke 40 mi NE
Why set aside Same college-town problem as Gainesville. VT engineering grads exit to NoVA/DC, RTP, Charlotte, Atlanta within months. Bivocational work in SW Virginia is thin; the metro can't hold a young-pro post-grad population long enough for a discipleship arc to take root.

Portland · PSU + Reed

Genuinely unreached — without a flagship anchor.
  • Portland State R1 (~22K undergrad), mid-tier urban university

  • Portland metro ~2.5M; lowest religious-affiliation rate of any major US metro (Pacific Northwest "None Zone")
  • AAPI ~7% metro
  • A2N-open
Why set aside No flagship public anchor (UO is in Eugene, 2h south, not Portland). Reed is too small. Carolina pipeline inert. Tech and corporate base lighter than Seattle or Bay Area.

Miami · University of Miami + FIU

An emerging finance hub with a different demographic shape.
  • UM top-50 private (~12K total), FIU R1 (~56K)

  • High religious-nones rate; tech industry growing fast
  • AAPI ~7%; A2N-open

St. Louis · Wash U

Elite anchor in a shrinking metro.

  • SLU mid-tier private; UMSL nearby
  • St. Louis metro ~2.8M, flat-to-declining over the last decade
  • Lowest cost of living among elite-anchor candidates
  • A2N-open
Why set aside WashU is too small to anchor a multi-campus thesis (~7.5K undergrad). St. Louis metro shrinkage is a structural headwind no other candidate carries. Carolina pipeline inert.
§ XI. The Verdict

Four paths.
One question.

The picture is in front of us. Each city presents a coherent shape of obedience, and the right answer turns less on which is "better" in the abstract than on where the missional need most fits this team's calling — and which trade-offs the team is built to bear for the long haul. What follows is the discernment framework, not the conclusion.

— Choose Atlanta if —

A Tech-anchored Midtown is where the Lord is sending us.

  • Georgia Tech as a deep, walking-distance anchor (with SCAD as a natural neighbor) is the work; broader multi-campus reach is grace, not premise
  • Walking alongside an existing AA ministry community matters more than entering a field with fewer co-laborers
  • The sister A2N plant in the metro feels like a brother work, not a constraint
  • The Carolina UNC network being live (drive / 90-min flight) is part of how the Lord is providing for the team
  • Long-arc discipleship (10-year disciples → bivocational co-laborers) is core to the calling
  • Higher cost is offset by deep F500 / Big Tech bivocational work that makes long faithful presence sustainable
— Choose Phoenix if —

A post-Christian Sun Belt field is where the Lord is sending us.

  • The greater missional need — a city the gospel is faintly heard in — matters more than the warmth of a Bible-Belt baseline
  • The two-anchor geometry the Lord set in place (ASU as mission field + GCU as leadership pipeline) is the gift the team is leaning on
  • ~25% lower cost of living gives the team a longer sustainable presence here
  • The team is willing to plant without a deep AA ministry community to walk with
  • Living among a stream of young adults still arriving in the Valley is part of the calling
  • Sending (plant + missions) matters as much to the vision as how many graduates stay
— Choose Charlotte if —

A banking-anchored, Carolina-live field is where the Lord is sending us.

  • The Carolina UNC network being literal — 2.5h drive to Chapel Hill — is the gift the team most wants to use
  • One real anchor (UNCC) is enough; the team isn't trying to be a multi-campus body
  • Banking depth (BoA, Truist, Honeywell, Lowe's) fits the bivocational rhythm
  • The most room of any city for the particular shape of work this team is sent to do — no existing church holds the integrated AA-led college-and-young-pro work here
  • South-suburb AA growth (Ballantyne / Concord) is a community to live among, not just minister to
  • Triangle A2N adjacency 2.5h up I-85 is a relationship to honor, not a blocker
— Choose New York if —

A least-reached, multi-campus Brooklyn field is where the Lord is sending us.

  • The greatest missional need — a young-adult field with low-single-digit evangelical density — outweighs every other consideration
  • Four campuses in one walking radius (Tandon, LIU, Pratt, St. Francis) is a multi-campus mission field most cities cannot offer
  • The team is built to give their lives here for ten years, not three — a body that stays against Brooklyn's transience
  • The two-wing bridge (undergrads mentoring high-schoolers; young pros anchoring the body) fits the team's distinctives and Brooklyn's geography
  • Brooklyn's historically Black neighborhoods (Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Bed-Stuy) are the cultural memory an AA-led plant should root in
  • The team is willing to plan a runway honest about the city's severe cost — and to walk in respect alongside the existing Brooklyn churches already at work

— Questions to sit with before deciding —

  1. Where is the missional need greatest — and where does that need most fit the shape of this team's calling? (Phoenix and New York carry the heaviest lostness; Atlanta and Charlotte sit inside warmer Bible-Belt baselines.)
  2. How much does academic seriousness of the anchor campus weigh? Top-tier R1 favors GT (Atlanta); NYU Tandon (NYC) is engineering-elite within the four-campus shape; ASU and UNCC are mid-tier with real strength.
  3. Is the brother A2N plant in Atlanta a gift to receive, or a relationship the team is not built to steward?
  4. How does the team weigh Carolina UNC network geometry against the missional need elsewhere? Atlanta and Charlotte keep the network live; Phoenix and New York do not.
  5. How long is the team committed for? Five years tilts toward Phoenix or Charlotte; ten or more makes Atlanta's long-arc discipleship and Brooklyn's "body that stays" both viable.
  6. Which cost is the team built to bear — Atlanta's saturation, Phoenix's pioneering, Charlotte's geography, or New York's severe cost of living?
  7. Where do the team's spouses, children, and aging-parent realities point?
  8. Which city is the Lord giving the team a peace and a love for — to live in, raise families in, and bury people in for a decade?
