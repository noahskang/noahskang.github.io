§ I. The Question

What's the shape of the call?

A bivocational team of ten, gathered from across the country. An Asian-American leadership shape. The pull of a major university campus and a vision for a midweek young-adult gathering at scale. These are the inputs.

The output is supposed to be a city, a neighborhood, and a strategy. But before we choose a city, we need to be honest about what's genuinely distinctive in this calling, and what the surrounding landscape will reward or punish.

This document started as a working comparison of the two strongest candidates — Atlanta and Phoenix — with a proposal for a Midtown-anchored strategy that took seriously what was already on the field, including a related AA-led plant already operating in the metro. It has since expanded to include Charlotte — a Carolina-pipeline-live banking metro with deep corporate density and an open lane on the team's specific posture. Several additional candidates were considered seriously and set aside; those are surfaced in the Alternatives tab with the structural reason each was excluded.

§ III. The Retention Question

Do they stay?

Three sending paths come out of every graduating class — local bivocational, plant elsewhere, missions. The first path requires that graduates stay in your metro. The other two don't. Retention math doesn't determine whether the flywheel works; it determines what proportion of it stays nearby to become the church's own sending base.

— Atlanta Pipeline —

Tech grad stays.

— Phoenix Pipeline —

ASU grad often leaves.

— Charlotte Pipeline —

UNCC grad stays — and imports more.

Atlanta — retention is structural.

The Tech "Yellow Jacket Mafia" is not a meme. Tech alone supports an estimated 33,000 Georgia jobs and contributes ~$5.3B to the state's economy annually. Atlanta hosts 17 Fortune 500 headquarters — Coca-Cola, Delta, Home Depot, UPS, Cox, Inspire Brands, Genuine Parts, NCR, Equifax, Aflac, Truist, Southern Co. — alongside major Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, and Mailchimp/Intuit footprints. Emory anchors a healthcare cluster with the CDC across the street.

  • Tech + Emory grads recruited heavily by local F500 / Big Tech ATL offices
  • Cost still permits new grads to live in Midtown / O4W / West Midtown
  • Deep young-professional culture: people stay 3 – 7 years post-grad
  • Implication: students discipled at 19 can become bivocational team at 23 — in the same metro, on the local sending base

Phoenix — retention is in transition.

Historically, ASU grads trended toward LA, Seattle, Austin, and the Bay Area for higher-paying tech work. That picture is changing rapidly: TSMC's Phoenix investment program (announced in stages from $40B in 2022 to roughly $165B by 2025), Intel's Ocotillo expansion, the broader East Valley semiconductor corridor, Amazon's massive operations footprint, and a swelling finance/insurance presence are creating reasons to stay that didn't exist five years ago.

  • AAPI tech grads increasingly retained by Chandler corridor employers
  • Cheap rent makes staying viable for 22-year-olds with internships only
  • But: post-grad ecosystem still less mature than Atlanta or DFW
  • Implication: the local-bivocational path is thinner here — the plant-elsewhere and missions paths still run normally

Charlotte — retention is the highest of the candidates.

Charlotte may be the strongest retention market in the comparison set. UNCC alumni cluster heavily inside the metro by default — UNCC is overwhelmingly an in-state, in-region school — and the banking sector (BoA, Truist, Wells Fargo, JPMorgan Chase Charlotte) recruits both directly from UNCC and from UNC, Duke, and other regional schools, pulling more young pros into the metro than UNCC itself sends out. Post-pandemic NYC and Bay-Area finance migration adds a second inflow channel.

  • UNCC's in-state student body retains in-region by default
  • Banking gravity (BoA HQ + Truist HQ) anchors finance careers
  • The metro is a net importer of young pros, not a net exporter
  • Implication: the local-bivocational path is the deepest of any candidate — Charlotte's flywheel can compound on imported talent as well as retained grads

01Campus Density

Tech is the anchor. The rest is upside.

Atlanta's multi-campus thesis is real, but more negotiable than the headline number suggests. Georgia Tech is the obvious centerpiece — 47,000 students, walking distance to Midtown — and is the only campus the plant has to plan around. Beyond Tech, the rest is upside, not premise.

SCAD Atlanta sits inside Midtown itself: a small but culturally adjacent student body the plant can absorb without extra logistics. Georgia State's downtown campus is one MARTA stop away, but its student profile skews older and commuter-heavy — a different demographic than the undergrad-formation play. Emory is a 15–25 minute drive east depending on traffic; reaching its roughly 7,000 undergraduates from a Midtown gathering is a stretch on most weeknights, and Emory already has well-developed religious-life infrastructure of its own. AUC is closer geographically but represents a culturally distinct institutional gravity.

The honest frame: plan for Tech-and-SCAD on a Tuesday night. GSU, Emory, and AUC become live questions only if the team has the bandwidth and a clear point of contact. Designing for the focused version is sharper than promising a five-campus reach the team cannot deliver.

02Asian American Reality

Mature, dense, and twenty miles away.

Atlanta has roughly twice the Asian American density of Phoenix metro and one of the fastest-growing AAPI communities in the South. Between 2013 and 2019 metro Atlanta added more than 75,000 AAPI residents — the 10th-largest absolute increase among US metros.

The catch: that density lives in Gwinnett (Duluth 22%, Suwanee 24%) and North Fulton (Johns Creek 29%). It is not in Midtown. The team will live, eat, and recruit in two different worlds — campus / Midtown by day, Buford Highway / Duluth on weekends. This is workable, and arguably advantageous, but it has to be designed for.

04The Campus Shelf

The para-church layer is thicker still.

The seven young-adult churches above are only the church side. The campus side — staffed para-church ministries living on Atlanta's college lawns — is denser: roughly thirty active chapters across Tech, Emory, GSU, the AUC, and SCAD Atlanta. Cru, InterVarsity, RUF, BCM, FCA, the UMC's Wesley Foundations, and the Catholic centers all run multi-campus operations with houses, staff, and decade-plus institutional memory. None of this is on the church-side scoreboard, but a Tuesday-night midweek gathering competes with all of it for student attention.

The shelf is not flat. Tech and Emory are the contested campuses; GSU and SCAD are lighter; the AUC runs on its own institutional gravity and is best treated as a partnership question, not an absorption one. The team's AA-led leadership shape and integrated college-to-young-pro pipeline are the real wedges here — neither is well-represented on the current shelf, especially at Tech.

05Runway

Liveable for ten bivocationals — but not cheap.

Midtown 1BR rent runs $1,900 – $2,200; West Midtown and Old Fourth Ward shave 10–15% off. Home Park and points west get cheaper still. Bivocational employment is genuinely abundant — Tech, Microsoft, Google ATL, Mailchimp/Intuit, Salesforce, Coca-Cola, Delta, Home Depot, NCR, Cox, Inspire Brands, plus the Emory / CDC / Emory Healthcare cluster on the east side. White-collar work for a high-caliber team is not a worry here.

06The Carolina Pipeline

An unfair advantage the team already has.

Several of the team did college ministry at UNC. That's not a footnote — it's a structural asset for an Atlanta plant in a way it simply isn't for a Phoenix one. Chapel Hill is a 6.5-hour drive or a 90-minute direct flight from Atlanta. There are daily nonstops. UNC has roughly 32K students, a deep evangelical campus-ministry tradition, and an alumni population in metro Atlanta that runs into the tens of thousands.

Three downstream implications:

i.
Team backfill.   UNC ministry alumni — the tier just behind your launch ten — are far more likely to consider relocating to Atlanta than to Phoenix. Years 2–5 recruiting has a real pool to draw from.
ii.
Summer pipeline back to Chapel Hill.   Easy summer trips for recruiting Carolina seniors who'd otherwise scatter. Easy joint events with whoever's still doing campus ministry there. Easy intern exchanges.
iii.
Future sister plants.   The same alumni network that feeds the Atlanta plant could seed future Southeast plants — Charlotte, Nashville, Raleigh-Durham — without building a recruiting funnel from scratch.

From Phoenix, this advantage essentially disappears. Chapel Hill to Phoenix is a five-hour flight with a layover; the casual drive-or-fly cadence that makes UNC a live partner from Atlanta becomes a planned, expensive logistics problem from the desert.

§ V. The Family Factor

Two ministries. Parallel, not overlapping.
Occasional collaboration.

An existing A2N AA-led plant — bridging Emory, Georgia Tech, and UGA from Duluth — works the same campuses this plant would be working. The realistic shape of that relationship isn't shared students. Asking a 19-year-old to be a full participant in two parallel ministries is unrealistic; the time and emotional bandwidth aren't there. What's actually workable is two parallel works, reaching different students, with deliberate collaboration at a few touchpoints each year.

Existing A2N plant — primary work

The campus fellowship.

  • Campus-embedded, BCM-style relational discipleship
  • Across Emory, GT, UGA — multi-school
  • Reaches its own pool of students through its own networks
  • Operates from Duluth — within the AA family network
The proposed plant — primary work

The local church.

  • Midtown-anchored church with its own college ministry
  • Reaches students through its gatherings, teams, friendships
  • Different student profile, different relational network
  • Friendly with A2N — not competing for the same names
§ VI. The Midtown Strategy

Plant in Midtown.
Bridge campus and city.

A college-and-young-professional church anchored in Midtown Atlanta — built first around Georgia Tech, with SCAD Atlanta as natural adjacency, and embedded in the post-grad neighborhood where 22–30s already live, work, and look for community. GSU is reachable by MARTA and Emory by car, but those are secondary work, not the premise.

— Pillar 01 —

Sunday gathering.
Midweek front door.

Sunday morning is for the church family — undergrads, young pros, a few young families. Tuesday or Wednesday night is the public-facing gathering: large, polished, evangelistic, where students bring friends.

— Pillar 02 —

College ministry as a
core arm.

Not a feeder, not an afterthought. A dedicated college pastor, weekly college community, semester-paced rhythms, fall and spring retreats. Students should know this church is for them — not just a stepping-stone to the young-pro thing.

— Pillar 03 —

Young-pro ministry as the
parallel arm.

Vocational discipleship, neighborhood small groups in West Midtown / O4W / Buckhead, marriage and family on-ramps, third-place hospitality. The rhythm of life after 22 is structurally different from college; programming has to reflect that.

— Pillar 04 —

AA sensibility,
multi-ethnic table.

Cultural touchstones, food, hospitality, leadership shape — but not a membership requirement. Tech and Midtown are too multi-ethnic to do anything else; the AA-led identity is the flavor, not the gate.

The six-year flywheel.

The model is faster than it sounds. A student arrives as a freshman, becomes a leader by sophomore or junior year, graduates ready to serve, and within a year or two of graduation is functioning as a kingdom worker — locally, in another US city, or overseas. Six years from welcome to deployment.

Tactic 01 — Venue

Anchor near Tech Square.

Walking distance for Georgia Tech and SCAD Atlanta students; one MARTA stop from GSU's downtown campus. Emory sits at the edge of practical reach for a Midtown gathering — plan for it as occasional, not standing. Either rent a midweek venue (Tabernacle, Center Stage, Variety Playhouse, hotel ballrooms) or partner with an existing Midtown church for shared space.

Cost order — $4–8K / month rented · $0 partnership
Tactic 02 — Cadence

Tuesday is the front door.

Worship + teaching + invitation-friendly, designed so undergrads can bring friends. Sunday is a smaller, family-feel gathering for members and regulars. Midweek can be larger than Sunday by design.

Pattern — large gathering + church family
Tactic 03 — College Pastor

Hire a college pastor from day one.

Not a "young adult" pastor who treats college as a sub-population. A dedicated leader for the 18-22 ministry: weekly college community, an embedded presence at Georgia Tech first (with SCAD as natural adjacency), fall/spring retreats, mentoring pipelines into the church's young-pro arm. GSU and Emory presence becomes a year-two question, only if a willing student leader emerges.

Lead — within first 12 months
Tactic 04 — Living Map

Team lives in Midtown / West Midtown / O4W.

Bivocational families need to be neighbors to the people they're reaching. Avoid the Duluth-commute trap that breaks most ATL young-adult ministries. Rent comes with the territory.

Cluster — 30308 / 30318 / 30312
Tactic 05 — Coordinate with the existing A2N plant

Parallel works, occasional collaboration.

Different students, different relational networks, different programs. The two works do not try to share members; the time math doesn't work. What they share: 2–3 collaboration points a year — a joint summer gathering, a leader training, a missions trip. Friendly cooperation, not entangled membership.

Cadence — quarterly leadership alignment + 2-3 joint events / year
Tactic 06 — Partner Church

Land an Atlanta sponsor.

Large gatherings of this kind work in part because they piggyback on a partner church's venue, 501(c)(3), and operations. Identify one large Atlanta church (Passion, Buckhead, Christ Covenant, Grace Midtown) willing to host or back the plant. This decision shapes everything else.

First conversations — months 1–3
Tactic 07 — Bivocational Slates

Pre-place the ten.

Before launch, line up jobs for all ten team members within Midtown commute radius. Tech roles at MSFT/Google/Mailchimp, healthcare at Emory, design and corporate at the Coca-Cola / Delta / Home Depot tier. Pre-placing is the survival difference.

Lead time — 6–12 months pre-launch
Tactic 08 — Measure What Matters

Count retained five-year disciples.

Not Tuesday-night attendance. Not Sunday baptisms alone. Track the retention curve from first-time visitor → small group → leader → bivocational worker. The flywheel is a ten-year compound machine; build instrumentation that measures the compounding.

KPI — # of 25-year-olds in vocational + ministry rhythm

01Two Anchor Schools

The largest single university in the country — plus a sleeper.

The first chapter of the Phoenix campus story is ASU. Tempe alone is 80,000 students, the largest single-campus enrollment in the United States. The second chapter, easy to miss from the outside, is Grand Canyon University — a 25,000-student residential Christian university 25 minutes northwest of Tempe, with the largest on-campus housing community of any private university in the country. Combined with ASU's downtown campus and the smaller West and Polytechnic sites, the count of accessible students approaches 130,000.

The two anchors play very different roles in the strategy. ASU is a vast secular mission field. GCU is a pre-formed Christian student body — and that distinction has structural implications we treat in Block 05.

03The Ministry Shelf

The college slice already has two strong incumbents.

Phoenix has serious churches across the family-and-Sunday side — Christ's Church of the Valley (CCV), Redemption Church, Dream City. On the college slice — which is where this plant would live — there are already two strong operations on Mill Avenue. Salt Company at ASU runs the most established midweek college gathering in the metro: a Salt Network plant out of Cornerstone Church (Ames), Thursday-night programming on or near campus, the closest Phoenix analogue to what Atlanta's young-adult incumbents do. Hope Phoenix — a Hope Church Movement plant led by Brian Smith — meets Sundays at the Marquee Theatre on Mill Avenue, walking distance to ASU, with an explicitly college-focused "Campus Changer" framing. Between them they cover the polished college midweek and the college-focused Sunday in Tempe.

The honest framing: the lane is less crowded than Atlanta, but it is not "open." The team's plant would be the third college-aware operation in the radius around ASU, not the first. The differentiation has to be real and concrete: an AA-led leadership shape, an integrated college-to-young-pro pipeline (most incumbents here are either pure-college like Salt and Hope, or pure-family-and-young-pro like CCV / Redemption — not both), and a multi-ethnic posture in a metro that does not yet have an established AA-led church plant of this kind. That is a distinct posture, but it is differentiation by depth and shape, not by absence.

The flip side: Phoenix is also less religious by baseline. There is no Bible-Belt warm pool to start with. Initial traction will look slower; downstream conviction is often deeper.

04Runway

The bivocational math works.

Tempe 1BR rent runs $1,500–$1,800; Chandler and Gilbert offer real family housing well under Atlanta numbers. Bivocational employment is real and growing — TSMC's Phoenix fabs, Intel Ocotillo, Honeywell, American Express, USAA, plus Amazon's massive operations footprint. For a ten-family team, the cost-of-living delta translates to roughly 18–24 extra months of runway versus Atlanta at the same fundraising level.

05The GCU Pipeline

A 25,000-student Christian campus down the road.

Twenty-five minutes from Tempe sits a structural asset most planters overlook: Grand Canyon University. 25,000 traditional residential students, the largest residential population of any private university in the country. Founded in 1949 as a Christian college, still explicitly Christian today, with a curriculum built around Christian-worldview formation and a campus culture that pushes against the broader cultural tide of college-age deconstruction.

The national pattern is the one to sit with: research from Barna, Lifeway, and Pew consistently shows that a majority of churched students — typically 60–70% by graduation — disengage from active Christian faith during their college years. GCU's internal surveys show the inverse: roughly 9 in 10 students report their faith is supported and developing through their time on campus. Those students are not the same evangelistic field as ASU. They are something else entirely.

i.
Pre-formed leaders, not seekers.   A church plant in Phoenix can recruit GCU students as student leaders — running small groups, leading worship, doing campus outreach at ASU — in their second or third year, with theology classes already done.
ii.
Bivocational backfill.   GCU graduates considering ministry, missions, or the marketplace are already pointed toward kingdom work. They are exactly the demographic a bivocational team needs to recruit from in years 2–5.
iii.
Sending base.   Many GCU students arrive intending to plant or go overseas. A church that can be their home base for 4–6 years and then send them is well-positioned to feed the second and third paths of the flywheel — plant elsewhere, missions — even where local-bivocational retention is weaker.

What's actually on the GCU campus.   The pipeline thesis lands harder once you see GCU's own ministry footprint up close. Three weekly chapel services average roughly 5,000 attendees combined. Life Groups — weekly student-led Bible studies — run in every residential living area on campus, staffed by five professional Spiritual Life staff and ~220 trained student leaders. The Center for Worship Arts feeds Canyon Worship and trains worship leaders by the cohort. A Christian-worldview core curriculum runs across all programs. External para-church organizations (Cru, RUF, InterVarsity) are functionally minimal on campus — the institution itself fills that lane.

The implication cuts both ways. Positively: GCU is genuinely producing trained student ministry leaders at scale, and a plant that lands a partnership with even a modest share of that flow has a real talent pipeline. Honestly, though, two frictions deserve to be named up front. Bandwidth — most upperclassmen who would be leadership candidates are already embedded in Life Group leadership, worship team, or outreach roles on their own campus, so off-campus recruitment is competing with existing investment, not filling empty time. Distance — 25 minutes to Tempe is fine for a Sunday or a major event, harder for casual mid-week presence. The pipeline is real; it is not friction-free, and the Block 07 strategy assumes a formal partnership channel rather than ad-hoc recruiting precisely because of these two.

The role-split is structurally clean: ASU is the mission field; GCU is the leadership pipeline. A weekly gathering near Tempe reaches the unchurched 80K; intentional partnership with GCU recruits and trains the leaders who reach them. This dual-anchor logic doesn't exist in Atlanta with anything like the same shape.

06The Reputation Shift

Improving and isolated — better than its reputation, not a flagship peer.

For two decades, ASU's national reputation was set by the Princeton Review's annual party-school list, which named ASU the country's #1 party school in 2002 — and the perception calcified. Reality has moved. Under President Michael Crow's "New American University" model (2002 forward), ASU has been ranked the #1 most innovative university in the United States by U.S. News for ten consecutive years, average incoming-freshman GPA has climbed past 3.5, and research expenditures now sit alongside the upper R1 tier. The Cronkite School of Journalism, the W. P. Carey School of Business, and the Ira A. Fulton Schools of Engineering all run nationally competitive programs.

Barrett, the Honors College, deserves a calibrated read. It is one of the country's largest and best-regarded public honors colleges (~7,100 students, average SAT ~1347, ~30% acceptance, top-rated by Public University Honors) and offers a self-contained residential community with its own dorms and curriculum. But the student-quality profile is closer to "strong public-honors" than "top-25 private" — average Barrett SAT runs 100–150 points below top-25 admit medians, and the cross-admit pool is more often UA Honors / UMD Honors / Penn State Schreyer than Vanderbilt or Duke. Real, valuable, and a meaningful talent layer at ASU; not a substitute for a flagship-tier anchor.

The harder structural fact for an academic-anchor-sensitive plant: ASU is academically isolated. The closest elite institution is Caltech, ~370 miles and a six-hour drive west. The closest in-state peer is the University of Arizona in Tucson (~110 miles), a solid mid-tier R1 but not elite. There is no nearby Emory-to-GT, Davidson-to-UNCC, Stanford-to-Berkeley, or UVA-to-VCU regional cluster. ASU stands alone in a way none of the other candidates do.

The campus today pulls two ways at once: residual social culture from a large state school, plus a fast-rising academic and research engine. For a college plant, that is genuinely interesting — there is a real apologetic and pastoral lane on a campus most ministries underestimate, especially within Barrett's intellectually serious slice. But for a team that has identified academic seriousness of the anchor as a meaningful criterion, ASU + Barrett reads as "improving public R1" rather than "flagship-tier anchor with elite peer ecosystem nearby."

07The Tempe Strategy

Anchor in Tempe. Recruit at GCU. Build for the Valley.

If Phoenix is the call, the shape of the play follows from the asymmetric pieces already on the table: a college slice with two incumbents but no AA-led, integrated college-and-young-pro plant (Salt Company holds the midweek; Hope Phoenix holds the college Sunday at the Marquee), a dual-anchor (ASU as mission field, GCU as leadership pipeline), the Chandler / Gilbert AA cluster, and a cost structure that lets a bivocational team breathe. Five tactical pillars:

i.
Anchor in Tempe — walking distance to ASU.   Sunday gathering and midweek front door both inside the Mill Avenue / Apache Boulevard radius. Note: the Marquee Theatre on Mill Ave is already held by Hope Phoenix on Sunday mornings, so the team's Sunday slot needs a different venue (Tempe Center for the Arts, a Mill Avenue hotel ballroom, the Tempe Music Garden, or similar) or a CCV / Redemption campus partnership. Midweek slots at the Marquee may still be in play. The play only works if students can walk or scooter from the dorms.
ii.
Build the GCU partnership in year one.   A formal MOU with a GCU campus pastor or Christian Studies faculty member to send juniors and seniors to lead at the ASU midweek, intern at the church, and stay through the bivocational pipeline post-graduation. This is the staffing lever that does not show up on any payroll line.
iii.
Plant the AA arm in Chandler.   A small-group / extension community in the Chandler corridor — not a second site in year one, but a deliberate node where AA tech families can find the church without driving to Tempe. By year three, this becomes a live candidate for a second campus.
iv.
Pre-place the ten in semis, ops, and finance.   TSMC and Intel Ocotillo are the obvious anchors; Honeywell, American Express, USAA, Amazon ops, and Charles Schwab round out the white-collar bivocational room. Engineers land at TSMC / Intel; PMs and ops at AmEx and Amazon; finance at Schwab. Pre-placement is the runway.
v.
Engineer for summer dispersal.   Phoenix's 110° summers empty Tempe — students go home, professionals travel. Build the calendar around it: lighter summer cadence, intentional formation summer (missions trips, leader retreats at higher altitude), a deliberate "back to ASU" launch every August. Don't fight the climate; design with it.

The honest version of the Phoenix bet: narrower in year one than Atlanta, with a structural ceiling that is higher because the AA-led, integrated college-and-young-pro posture is not what the existing incumbents (Salt Company and Hope Phoenix on the college side, CCV / Redemption / Dream City on the family-and-Sunday side) are running. The cost is a slower start and a thinner partner ecosystem. The upside is a defensible, decade-long position in a metro the country is still moving toward.

One real anchor — and a cluster of small support.

Charlotte's college math is honest: UNC Charlotte is the anchor and almost the only anchor at scale. Fall 2025 set a record 32,207 students (26,213 undergrad, 5,994 graduate) — a real R1-tier population, but a commuter-leaning campus on the city's northeast edge, ~12 miles from Uptown. 02The Suburban AA Boom

City share modest, suburb growth real and fast.

Charlotte's Asian American share city-wide is ~6.5% — comparable to Atlanta's metro figure. The deeper story is what's happening in the south suburbs. Ballantyne, Marvin, Concord, and the Cary-equivalent ring of Mecklenburg / Union / Cabarrus counties have seen a sustained Indian-American population boom over the last decade, driven by the same school-quality migration pattern that built Johns Creek and Duluth in Atlanta a generation earlier. The pattern is real enough that local school districts have publicly tracked it as a demographic event.

The geometry: AA density lives 15–25 minutes south of Uptown (Ballantyne, Pineville, Marvin) and 25–30 minutes northeast (Concord). UNC Charlotte sits roughly between these poles but closer to neither — a familiar Atlanta-style "live in two worlds" pattern, less acute than the 30-minute Gwinnett gap but still real.

03The Ministry Shelf

Elevation sets the gravity. Past that, the lane is real.

Charlotte's young-adult shelf is shaped less by saturation and more by one outsized incumbent. Elevation Church (~17K weekly attendance across 20 locations as of 2024) is the metro's gravitational center for under-40 churchgoers, and its cultural footprint shapes how Charlotte Gen Z reads "church" in ways that have no real Atlanta or Phoenix analogue. Past Elevation, the shelf is genuinely lighter than Atlanta: Forest Hill, Mecklenburg Community Church, Calvary Church (with explicit 18–25 college-and-young-adult programming), Freedom House (its MVMNT young-adult community runs 18–29), and Covenant Presbyterian (Myers Park — established young-adult ministry) round out the field.

What's missing on the existing shelf — a polished, college-anchored midweek gathering with an integrated college-to-young-pro pipeline and an AA-led identity — is precisely the team's distinctive thesis. Charlotte does not have a Living Room ATL analogue. It does not have a Passion-scale young-adult magnet. The lane is not empty (Calvary and Freedom House each hold pieces of it), but it is meaningfully more open than Atlanta or Phoenix's ASU radius.

04The Campus Shelf

Para-church coverage at UNCC is thinner than Tech.

Where Atlanta's Tech anchor sits under nine-plus para-church chapters, UNC Charlotte's documented Christian campus presence runs leaner: Cru, RUF (PCA), Baptist Campus Ministries, and a Catholic Campus Ministry center. That is real coverage but not the multi-decade institutional density Tech has accumulated. Davidson's religious life is more chapel-and-chaplaincy in tradition; Queens has institutional Presbyterian roots. JCSU operates within the AME Zion HBCU religious-life pattern, which is a culturally distinct shelf.

The strategic read: UNCC has more room for a new church-led college presence than Tech does. The constraint is less competitive saturation and more campus geography — an off-campus midweek gathering near UNCC has to reckon with a 12-mile drive from Uptown / South End where most young professionals will live. A South Park or NoDa anchor with a UNCC bus or shuttle is not unlike The Living Room ATL's metro logistics, and is probably the realistic shape of the play.

05Runway

Banking corporate depth — between Atlanta and Phoenix on cost.

Charlotte holds seven Fortune 500 headquarters in-region and 19 Fortune 1000 — Bank of America (#17, $192B revenue), Lowe's (#52), Honeywell (#119), Nucor (#140), Duke Energy (#144), Truist (#168), and Sonic Automotive — alongside a dense second-tier of regional banking, energy, and logistics. The bivocational job market is one of the best in the Southeast for finance, technology-in-banking, energy, and regulated industries; less broad than Atlanta's seventeen-F500 mix, but deep where it counts.

Cost: Uptown / South End / Plaza Midwood 1BR rent runs $1,650 – $2,000 — modestly cheaper than Midtown Atlanta and modestly more than Tempe. Bivocational math is workable; the Carolina-pipeline geometry plus banking corporate depth is the unique combination here.

06The Carolina Pipeline

The most literal version of the Carolina advantage.

Chapel Hill is 2.5 hours by car from Charlotte, with daily nonstop flights between CLT and RDU. The team's UNC ministry alumni network is geographically nearer than from Atlanta, and the cultural distance is shorter — Charlotte and the Triangle share a labor market, a college sports rivalry that doubles as a recruiting funnel, and a steady migration of UNC-and-Duke graduates into Charlotte's banking sector. UNC's metro Charlotte alumni population runs into the high tens of thousands.

One nuance worth naming up front: the Triangle is itself an A2N footprint (UNC, NC State, Meredith all operate as Acts 2 Fellowship). A team coming out of the UNC ministry orbit and planting in Charlotte will have an A2N sister plant 2.5 hours up I-85, and downstream recruiting from the Triangle has to be calibrated against that. This is a constraint, not a blocker — A2N's Triangle work and an independent AA-led plant in Charlotte are not direct competitors — but the team should walk into the relationship rather than around it.

07Banking Capital Character

The post-pandemic second financial city.

Charlotte spent the 2010s as a regional banking center; the 2020s have promoted it. Bank of America headquarters anchors the Uptown skyline (Trade & Tryon), but the deeper shift is the post-pandemic migration of finance and tech-in-finance roles out of New York and San Francisco. JPMorgan Chase's expanded Charlotte footprint, Truist's HQ relocation completion, Lowe's Tech Hub buildout in South End, Honeywell's HQ move from New Jersey, and the steady accretion of fintech (LendingTree, AvidXchange) and consulting (the Big Four all hold meaningful Charlotte offices) have pulled a specific demographic into the city: 24–32-year-old finance / tech / consulting professionals who took NYC-comp roles into a Charlotte cost basis. This is the demographic Atlanta's Midtown plant would also reach — but in Charlotte they arrive in greater concentration, with a clearer mental model of "the city I moved to for work," and without Atlanta's sprawl.

Where they live: South End (the Blue Line light-rail corridor, easily Charlotte's densest young-pro neighborhood — luxury midrise, breweries, food halls), Plaza Midwood / NoDa (the older, more design-forward, more arts-leaning side of the same demographic), Wesley Heights / Camden (newer, west-of-Uptown, faster-growing), and the southern edge of Dilworth. UNC Charlotte's main campus is 12 miles northeast of these, which is the dossier's structural complication — but the same students travel into Uptown / South End for internships, jobs, and social life routinely, so the venue logic is "where 22–30s live, with a UNCC bridge" rather than "near campus."

08The South End Strategy

Anchor in South End. Build the UNCC bridge in year one.

If Charlotte is the call, the shape of the play follows from the city's own geography. South End is to Charlotte's young-pro density what Midtown is to Atlanta's — the natural anchor neighborhood. The complication is that no single Charlotte neighborhood holds both the under-30 density and a major undergraduate population. South End has the young pros. UNCC has the students. A workable plant uses South End as the church anchor and builds a deliberate UNCC bridge as a year-one priority.

i.
Anchor in South End / Camden.   Sunday gathering and midweek front door both inside the Blue Line corridor. Venue options: a converted warehouse along Camden Rd, a Plaza Midwood theater, a NoDa flexible space, or partnership with an Uptown / South End church (Forest Hill, a Calvary site, an emerging Anglican parish) for shared facilities. The goal is walking distance for South End / Camden / Wesley Heights residents and a 10-minute light-rail ride for Plaza Midwood / NoDa.
ii.
Build the UNCC bridge as a year-one priority.   Hire a college pastor with explicit responsibility for UNCC. A weekly campus presence (Bible study or college community meeting) at or adjacent to UNCC, plus a Sunday shuttle-or-rideshare network into the South End service. UNCC student leaders mentored into year-two leadership of a college community that meets on campus and integrates with the South End church family. This is the structurally hardest piece — the 12-mile gap between South End and UNCC requires intentional logistics from day one, not a year-three afterthought.
iii.
Plant the AA arm in Ballantyne or Concord (year two-plus).   The South End core is multi-ethnic by default (the migration shape Charlotte attracts is not predominantly AA). The AA family-and-stay piece lives 25 minutes south in Ballantyne / Marvin or 25 minutes northeast in Concord. A small-group / extension community in one of those corridors — not a year-one priority — sets up a year-three second-campus question without overstretching launch.
iv.
Pre-place the ten in finance, fintech, and corporate.   BoA, Truist, Wells Fargo (still has a major regional footprint), JPMorgan Chase Charlotte, LendingTree, AvidXchange, the Big Four. Lowe's Tech Hub for engineering. Honeywell for hardware/operations. Duke Energy for the utility lane. The bivocational job market here is genuinely deep for finance / consulting / corporate-tech profiles; light for traditional Big Tech (Charlotte is not a Bay Area / NYC engineering market).
v.
Calibrate the Triangle / A2N relationship up front.   A2N's Triangle plant is 2.5 hours away. Recruiting overlap is real (UNC ministry alumni who relocate to Charlotte for finance work). Run an explicit conversation in months 1–3 about how the two works coexist — joint summer events, leader exchanges, no shared-membership trap, the same friendly-not-entangled posture that the Atlanta dossier proposes for the existing A2N plant in that metro.

The honest version of the Charlotte bet: cleanest open-lane fit on the team's profile, with a structural geography problem (one anchor campus 12 miles from where young-pros live) that has to be solved deliberately rather than ignored. The upside is a defensible AA-led, integrated college-and-young-pro plant in a metro the country is moving to, with the Carolina pipeline geographically live and the bivocational job market genuinely deep on the finance side.

— Where Charlotte wins —

  • Carolina pipeline is geographically literal — 2.5h drive to Chapel Hill, daily nonstops
  • The cleanest open lane on the team's profile — no incumbent runs the integrated college-and-young-pro AA-led play here
  • Banking corporate depth = strong bivocational job market, especially in finance / fintech
  • South suburb AA growth is real and accelerating — built-in demographic tailwind
  • No A2N footprint in Charlotte itself
  • Cost between Atlanta and Phoenix — modest savings vs. Midtown

— Where Charlotte costs you —

  • Only one true anchor campus (UNCC) vs. Atlanta's Tech-and-SCAD walking radius
  • UNCC sits 12 miles from where young professionals live — venue logistics are non-trivial
  • Elevation Church's gravitational pull shapes how Gen Z reads "church" in this metro
  • Triangle is A2N — sister-plant adjacency 2.5h away requires deliberate calibration
  • AA ministry partner ecosystem is younger and shallower than Atlanta's
  • South-suburb AA density is split between two corridors (S. Charlotte vs. Concord), not a single Gwinnett-style cluster

§ X. The Verdict

Three paths.
One question.

The data is on the table. Each city has a coherent thesis, and the right answer depends less on which is "better" in the abstract than on which trade-offs the team is built to absorb. What follows is the framework — not the conclusion.

— Choose Atlanta if —

A Tech-anchored Midtown is the call.

  • Georgia Tech as a deep, walking-distance anchor (with SCAD adjacency) is the play; broader multi-campus reach is upside, not premise
  • An AA ministry ecosystem to belong to and partner with matters more than landing in a less-saturated lane
  • The existing A2N plant in the metro feels like a partnership opportunity, not a constraint
  • The UNC ministry pipeline being live (drive / 90-min flight) is part of the recruiting math
  • Long retention compounding (10-year disciples → bivocational team) is core to the vision
  • Higher cost is offset by higher F500 / Big Tech bivocational job density
— Choose Phoenix if —

The open lane is the call.

  • Building something that doesn't yet exist matters more than slotting into a scene
  • The dual-anchor logic (ASU as field + GCU as pipeline) is the strategic asymmetry you want
  • ~25% lower cost of living changes the bivocational math meaningfully
  • The team is willing to pioneer without an established AA partner network
  • Riding a fast-growing metro's demographic wave is part of the strategic bet
  • Sending (plant + missions) matters as much to the vision as local retention
— Choose Charlotte if —

A banking-anchored, Carolina-live play is the call.

  • The Carolina pipeline being literal — 2.5h drive to Chapel Hill — is the asymmetry you want most
  • One real anchor (UNCC) is enough; the team isn't optimizing for multi-campus density
  • Banking corporate depth (BoA, Truist, Honeywell, Lowe's) fits the bivocational profile
  • The cleanest open lane on the team's profile — no incumbent runs the integrated AA-led college-and-young-pro play here
  • South-suburb AA growth (Ballantyne / Concord) feels like Gwinnett ten years ago
  • Triangle A2N adjacency 2.5h up I-85 is workable, not a blocker

— Questions to sit with before deciding —

  1. What is genuinely non-negotiable about the calling — academic anchor strength, multi-campus reach, less-saturated lane, or Carolina-pipeline geometry?
  2. How much does academic seriousness of the anchor campus weigh? Top-tier R1 favors GT (Atlanta); ASU and VCU are mid-tier with real strength; UNCC's commuter-leaning mid-tier profile is the binding constraint on Charlotte under this filter.
  3. Is the partnership with the existing A2N plant a feature of the Atlanta thesis, or could it become a structural tension?
  4. How does the team weigh Carolina-pipeline geometry against the saturation cost? Atlanta and Charlotte preserve the pipeline; Phoenix doesn't.
  5. How long is the team committed for? Five years favors Phoenix's or Charlotte's open lane; fifteen favors Atlanta's compounding.
  6. Which trade-off is the team built to absorb — higher saturation, higher pioneer cost, or sister-plant adjacency?
  7. Where does the team's spouses, children, and aging-parent infrastructure point?
  8. Which city does the team actually want to live in for a decade?
